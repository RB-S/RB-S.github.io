Credit Risk Analytics

Design a strategy to identify right customers during credit application phase

A leading credit card provider gets thousands of credit card applicants every year. But in the past few years, it has experienced an increase in credit loss.The CEO believes that the best strategy to mitigate credit risk is to 'acquire the right customers'.

We will use socio-demographic data provided by applicants during the credit application phase like age, gender, income, marital status, etc and credit bureau data to determine the factors affecting credit risk, create strategies to mitigate the acquisition risk and assess the financial benefit derived from the project.

The idea is to develope a predictive model to predict the likelihood of credit default by the applicants. We will identify the potential defaulters during application phase so that such customers are rejected and right customers are provided the credit card. This in turn will minimise the credit loss.

The standard process followed in analytics projects is:

  1. Business Understanding
  2. Data Understanding
  3. Model Development
  4. Model Evaluation
  5. Recommendations

Business Understanding

The objective is to identify right customers during credit application phase so that credit facility to the potential bad customers can be avoided and credit loss can be minimised.

Data Understanding

The data files provided by CredX are 1. Demographic data.csv 2. Credit Bureau data.csv

Demographic_data <- read.csv("Demographic data.csv", stringsAsFactors = F)
CB_data <- read.csv("Credit Bureau data.csv", stringsAsFactors = F)
# Replace dots in col name with underscore
colnames(Demographic_data) <- stringr::str_replace_all(colnames(Demographic_data), c("\\." = "_"))
colnames(CB_data) <- stringr::str_replace_all(colnames(CB_data), c("\\." = "_" , "\\.." = "_" ))

Demographic data has 71295 observations,i.e, we have 71295 applicants with 12 attributes like age, income, residence, gender, profession etc.

Credit Bureau data has 71295 observations with 19 attributes like DPD in 12 months, DPD in 6 months, outstanding balance, Average credit utilization etc.

The attribute "performance tag" represents whether the applicant has gone 90 days past due or worse in the past 12-months (i.e. defaulted) after getting a credit card.It thus contains only two values:-- 1 or 0 (1 means default and 0 means non-default) . It is called the target attribute since that is what we want to predict using the other attributes.

#--Merge demographic and credit Bureau data to create master dataset
CreditData <- dplyr::inner_join(Demographic_data, CB_data, by = "Application_ID")

Model Developement

We will build few models to predict the customers who are likely to default. To estimate the parameter coefficients we will train the model on some part of the data, called as training data. We will use rest of the data to test or evaluate the model, called as test data.

For our purpose we will use 70% data to train the model and rest 30% to test the model performance.

masterData_bin <- CreditData[,c(1,3:5,7:12,30:37,39:52,55)]

Lets check which are the important variables. To identify the important variable there are known different approaches like using varImp function from caret package, varImpPlot from randomForest package etc. For our purpose we will use Information package to derive Information value of each variable. The variable with highest information value will be most important one.

# Weight of Evidence(WOE), Information Value(IV) and Efficiency
library(Information)
infoTables <- Information::create_infotables(data = masterData_bin[,-1], y = "Performance_Tag")
infoTables$Summary$IV <- round(infoTables$Summary$IV * 100,2)

From the plot we notice that the Average credit utilization has the highest informaton value. Hence it is the most important variable followed by Open Trade, Outstanding balance etc.

Create a dataset with original values replaced with WoE values

Now we need to check how good is over model at classifying the defaulters from non-defaulters. For this we will plot lift cart which measures the performance of classification model.

Lift Chart

Lift charts compares the results one can expect from the predictive classification model compared to the result without a model. The model lift reflects the improvement in model performance (increase in true positive rate) when compared to random performance. Lift chart shows the additional response rate that would be achieved through the developed model.

KS chart

Now lets plot KS chart. This chart indicates the score/probability band where separate between Goods (or Event) and Bads (or Non-event) is maximum. KS statistics measures the discriminatory power of a model.

ks_stat(eNet.testSubset.30$lr.oriDefault.Status,eNet.testSubset.30$lr.predicted.prob)
## [1] 0.2692
# plot
ks_plot(ifelse(test.30$Performance_Tag == "no",0,1 ), pred.eNetModel.rose)

KS value of 26.92% is neither good nor bad. So the model identifed ~76% of potential defaulters from 50% of credit applicants. The important variables like Average credit utilizations, Outstanding Balance, Open Trade etc should be given more weightage and scrutinized while considering credit applications.

Next step would be to build application score card using the final model to identify the cut-off score below which customers can be rejected, perform reject referencing to check how the model would have performed on rejected applications, check model performance on through the door (TTD) applications, analyze financial benefit derived from the model etc.

On comparing the average credit loss with and without the model, we found that by identifying 76% of potential defaulters from 50% of credit applicants, the model cut down the revenue loss by 70% which is a significant improvement.
